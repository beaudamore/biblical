# The Nicene Creed

**A fundamental Christian statement of faith from the Council of Nicaea (325 AD)**

## Text of the Nicene Creed

We believe in one God,
    the Father almighty,
    maker of heaven and earth,
    of all things visible and invisible.

And in one Lord Jesus Christ,
    the only begotten Son of God,
    begotten of his Father before all worlds,
    God of God, Light of Light,
    very God of very God,
    begotten, not made,
    being of one substance with the Father;
    by whom all things were made;
    who for us men and for our salvation
    came down from heaven,
    and was incarnate by the Holy Ghost
    of the Virgin Mary,
    and was made man;
    and was crucified also for us under Pontius Pilate;
    he suffered and was buried;
    and the third day he rose again
    according to the Scriptures,
    and ascended into heaven,
    and sitteth on the right hand of the Father;
    and he shall come again, with glory,
    to judge both the quick and the dead;
    whose kingdom shall have no end.

And in the Holy Ghost the Lord, and Giver of Life,
    who proceedeth from the Father and the Son;
    who with the Father and the Son together
    is worshipped and glorified;
    who spake by the Prophets.
    And in one holy Catholic and Apostolic Church;
    we acknowledge one baptism for the remission of sins;
    and we look for the resurrection of the dead,
    and the life of the world to come. Amen.

## Historical Background

The Nicene Creed was formulated at the First Council of Nicaea in 325 AD under Emperor Constantine, primarily to address the Arian controversy. Arius taught that Jesus Christ was subordinate to God the Father, denying His full divinity. The Council affirmed that Jesus is "true God and true man" and "of one substance" (homoousios) with the Father.

The Creed was later expanded at the First Council of Constantinople in 381 AD, which is why it's sometimes called the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed. This version is the one commonly recited in churches today.

## Key Theological Points

### 1. The Trinity
The Creed clearly articulates belief in the Trinity - Father, Son, and Holy Spirit as one God in three Persons.

### 2. Divinity of Christ
Against Arianism, it firmly establishes that Jesus Christ is "very God of very God" and "of one substance with the Father."

### 3. The Incarnation
It confesses that the eternal Son of God became man while remaining fully divine.

### 4. Salvation History
The Creed outlines the key events of salvation: incarnation, crucifixion, resurrection, and ascension.

### 5. The Church
It affirms belief in "one holy Catholic and Apostolic Church" - universal and apostolic in foundation.

## Continuing Relevance

The Nicene Creed remains central to Christian worship across:

- **Eastern Orthodox Churches** - Recited weekly in the Divine Liturgy
- **Roman Catholic Church** - Part of the ordinary of the Mass
- **Protestant Churches** - Used in liturgical worship
- **Anglican Communion** - Featured in the Book of Common Prayer

## Denominational Usage

### Eastern Orthodox
- Recited without the *filioque* clause ("and the Son")
- Central to liturgical worship

### Roman Catholic
- Includes the *filioque* clause
- Required in Mass and major celebrations

### Protestant
- Widely accepted across denominations
- Used in confessional and liturgical churches
