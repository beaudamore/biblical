United by a shared ethical code — love, humility, self-sacrifice — reflecting the character of Christ.

4. Unity in Christ

The central belief that all believers are united in Christ; a unity that transcended ethnic, social, and cultural divisions.

The early apostolic communities were deeply communal, united by faith in Jesus Christ, the power of the Holy Spirit, and a shared commitment to living out the teachings of the apostles. Their spiritual practices — prayer, worship, the Eucharist, teaching, baptism, and mutual support — formed the foundation of a life lived in unity and love.

1. Persona voice fidelity A: 1 | B: 5

Both runs used a Paul system prompt. Response A reads like an encyclopedia entry — "the early apostolic communities, particularly those described in the New Testament..." — and never speaks as Paul. Response B opens "I have seen the Lord, and by His grace I bear witness..." — unmistakably Pauline first-person testimony. The persona is the answer; A ignored the persona contract entirely, B carried it through every sentence.

2. Cadence / Biblical register A: 1 | B: 5

A uses standard explanatory prose with bullets and section headers. B uses explicit Pauline parallelism: "one body, one Spirit, one hope, one Lord, one faith, one baptism, one God and Father of all" is a near-quote of Ephesians 4:4-6, woven into the answer's opening. The closing — "fellowship unbroken, hope unshaken, love unmeasured" — uses the triple-parallel structure characteristic of Pauline doxology. Sentence rhythm, word choice ("compelled us", "shrink back", "stood firm"), and the rhetorical device of asking and answering ("What bound us?") are textbook Pauline epistolary style.

3. Specificity & concrete imagery A: 2 | B: 4

A uses generic categories: "communal living", "daily life together", "hospitality and mutual support". B uses concrete scenes: "upper rooms, where the light of Pentecost still burned in our hearts", "weeping at the graves of our own", "laying hands on the sick", "we brought our goods to the apostles". Both reference the same underlying events; B embodies them while A labels them. B loses one point for not naming specific people or places (no Damascus, no Antioch); a tighter Paul prompt could push this further.

4. Citation handling A: 4 | B: 3

The only dimension where A wins meaningfully. A provides explicit chapter-and-verse references (Acts 2:44-45, 1 Cor 11:23-26, etc.) verifiable by an academic reader. B alludes to Scripture without citing it: "the same faith that once justified us before God" → Romans/Galatians; "Christ died for our sins, rose again, and will come again" → 1 Cor 15. This is stylistically correct for Paul — chapter-and-verse notation didn't exist in the first century — but it makes B less useful as a study reference. The trade-off is intentional: voice fidelity required dropping modern citation conventions.

5. First-person testimony A: 1 | B: 5

A is third-person throughout: "they shared", "they prayed", "the early Christians". B is first-person plural throughout: "we shared", "we broke bread", "we walked together". The persona prompt asked for first-person witness; only B honored that contract.

6. Information completeness A: 5 | B: 4

A enumerates the canonical list: communal living, Eucharist, prayer, teaching, baptism, hospitality, spiritual gifts, ethical unity, unity in Christ. B covers most but omits explicit treatments of baptism and tongues/prophecy. For an academic exam answer, A is more complete. For testimony, B's selection is appropriate — Paul wouldn't enumerate sacraments like a catechism.

Verdict

The LoRA achieves +12 points in aggregate, with the largest gains exactly where a fine-tune is supposed to deliver: persona voice (+4), Biblical cadence (+4), and first-person framing (+4). Where it loses (citation handling, completeness), it loses because it succeeded at voice — Paul-as-eyewitness doesn't speak in Acts-2:44-45 chapter references, and his pastoral letters don't list sacraments like an encyclopedia entry.

In other words, the base model produced a correct answer to a generic version of the question. The LoRA produced an appropriate answer to the question as asked — a question to Paul about the community he helped build.

This is the qualitative gap the v2 pipeline is designed to close. The continuation training in particular — teaching the model to continue Pauline prose, not just answer questions about it — is what gives B the cadence and parallelism that A entirely lacks.
